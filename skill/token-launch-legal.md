# Token Launch Legal Guide

## The most important question: is your token a security?

Getting this wrong means SEC enforcement, forced refunds, and potential criminal liability.
Getting it right means you can list on exchanges, raise freely, and sleep at night.

---

## The Howey Test (US)

A token is likely a security if it involves:
1. **An investment of money** — people pay for it
2. **In a common enterprise** — the project/protocol
3. **With expectation of profits** — buyers expect price appreciation
4. **From the efforts of others** — a core team drives the value

If all 4 are true → your token is likely a security under US law.

**The key lever founders control:** #4 — "efforts of others."
The more decentralized and functional your protocol *before* the token launch, the weaker the securities argument.

---

## Token types and their legal risk profile

### Utility tokens (lower risk)
- Have real, immediate use within the protocol
- Not marketed as investments
- Protocol is already functional at launch
- Examples: gas tokens, governance tokens with actual utility, access tokens

### Governance tokens (medium risk)
- Pure voting power with no economic rights = lower risk
- Voting power + fee revenue sharing = higher risk
- The SEC has targeted governance tokens that look like profit-sharing

### Security tokens (high risk — must register)
- Explicitly represent equity, revenue share, or debt
- Require SEC registration or valid exemption (Reg D, Reg S, Reg A+)
- Can be done legally but adds massive compliance overhead

### Memecoins (variable risk)
- No promises, no team roadmap = generally lower securities risk
- Risk: if a team is pumping it, market manipulation laws apply
- Rug pulls = fraud regardless of "not a security" claims

## Safe token launch checklist (2026)

Before launching any token:

- [ ] Get a written legal opinion from a crypto-specialized securities lawyer
- [ ] Ensure your protocol has real utility *before* the token launches
- [ ] Do not sell tokens to US persons without proper exemptions
- [ ] Avoid language like "investment," "returns," "profit," "yield" in marketing
- [ ] Do not promise a token price or imply price appreciation
- [ ] Use a Cayman Foundation or similar non-US entity as the token issuer
- [ ] Implement a vesting schedule for team tokens (minimum 1-year cliff)
- [ ] Do not airdrop to US persons without legal review
- [ ] Document all decisions and legal opinions — paper trail matters

## Geofencing US persons

Most Solana projects geofence the US at launch to avoid SEC jurisdiction:
- Block US IP addresses from token sale frontend
- Require users to confirm they are not US persons
- Use Cayman or BVI entity as issuer
- Do not attend US conferences to pitch the token

This does not make you immune — but it significantly reduces exposure.

---

## Reg D / Reg S exemptions (if you want US investors)

**Reg D 506(c):** Sell to accredited US investors only, with verification. No general solicitation before verification. File Form D with SEC.

**Reg S:** Sell only to non-US persons, offshore. Common for initial token sales from Cayman entities.

**Reg A+:** Raise up to $75M from the public including non-accredited US investors. Requires SEC qualification (6–12 months, expensive).

## The SEC's current stance (2026)

- The SEC under current leadership has been more crypto-friendly than 2023–2024
- But enforcement actions for clear securities violations continue
- Safe harbor proposals (like Commissioner Peirce's "Token Safe Harbor") have not passed into law
- Best practice: still get legal counsel, still geofence, still avoid "investment" language

---

## Exchanges and legal requirements

For listing on major CEXs (Binance, Coinbase, Kraken):
- They will ask for your legal opinion letter
- They will ask about token allocation and vesting
- They will screen for sanctions exposure (OFAC)
- Coinbase specifically requires a Coinbase Legal questionnaire

For Solana DEXs (Jupiter, Raydium, Orca):
- No legal review required to list
- But legal liability still exists for the token issuer
